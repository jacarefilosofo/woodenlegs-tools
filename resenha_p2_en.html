The Chronicle

Chapter 1 — The AI Picks the Teams

For the first time in Woodenlegs history, the teams were not chosen by a human being. They were chosen by a debate — a debate between a man and a machine.

The problem started on Wednesday, when president Amador summoned his personal Artificial Intelligence to build the teams. The AI, like a good employee, presented two options: the Old Metric, based on database ratings that only Amador decided, and the WoodenRichter Scale, based on the team's own vote — where every player ranked all their teammates in Impact, Speed, and Stamina.

The difference? In the old metric, Amador was the sole judge. In the WoodenRichter, the truth belongs to the collective. The AI ran 24,310 possible team combinations, tested goalkeeper constraints, separated the top three players (Wesley, Arthur, and Barba) onto different teams, and found the perfect balance: Brasil WR 11.24 vs Italia WR 11.26. A difference of 0.02. Mathematically, the fairest match ever assembled on this field.

Joel and Mano would like a word.

Chapter 2 — The Clock Doesn't Lie

Another first tonight: Arrival Tracking. For the first time ever, the Súmula recorded the exact arrival time of each player. 13 warriors right on time at 19:05. Glaydston, one minute late — probably tying his boots. Arthur, a respectable 19:10.

And then... silence. Until 19:34, when a shadow enters the field. It's Guilherme. Head down, not a single word. Nobody understood why he was late. Nobody asked. He just looked at the ground and went after both teams. Silent. Lethal.

At 19:39, Barba arrives. Six hours of driving. Straight from work. Deep bags under his eyes. Body begging for a bed, but heart begging for a goal. Spoiler: the body almost won.

Chapter 3 — Twenty-Three Minutes of Nothing

19:05. Kickoff. 17 players. Two goals. Zero balls inside them.

19:08 — nothing. 19:12 — nothing. 19:15 — absolutely nothing. 19:20 — everyone was starting to wonder if someone brought a real ball or a styrofoam one. 19:26 — theories emerged that the goal had an invisible force field. The group chat updated: "0 x 0, 8 minutes in." "12 min." "15 min 0 x 0." "18 min." "20 min 0 x 0." The messages kept getting shorter because hope was dying.

Until, at 19:28, a miracle.

Chapter 4 — Luigi Finally Found the Ball

Luigi, the same Luigi who practiced at home with his mom using a sock ball in his bedroom, finally — FINALLY — hit the target. Brasil 1 x 0. The desert was over. The 23-minute drought found its oasis.

Two minutes later, Fernando unleashes a backheel goal. A backheel. The man who said after the game that he played yesterday on a full-size pitch "and it's way easier there, you don't have to run as much — what a workout this is, I'm dead" — scored a backheel at the pelada. 1 x 1.

And then the sky fell on Brasil.

19:33 — Brocha. 19:35 — Arthur on loan to Italia. 19:36 — Arthur on loan again. 19:37 — Lucas. In nine minutes, it went from 1-0 to 1 x 5. Joel and Mano were already calling it a done deal.

Chapter 5 — The Comeback

But then Luigi shows up again. 19:41 — goal. 19:42 — another goal. Two in one minute. Suddenly the kid who trained with a sock ball became Brasil's top scorer. 3 x 5.

19:46 — Arthur scores for Brasil (on the right team this time). 4 x 5.

19:52 — Roger. 5 x 5. TIED. From 1-5 to 5-5 in 15 minutes.

Joel and Mano, who were absolutely sure the game was won, started to realize that maybe, just maybe, the teams were actually balanced. But complaining is easier than admitting. So they complained.

Chapter 6 — The Screamer and the Capoeira Artist

19:56 — Eric, who apparently stored up energy for several years by not running so he could spend it all in one night, unleashes a shot into the top corner. A screamer. A real screamer. The kind where you stop the game and clap. 5 x 6 Italia.

And Fernando? Fernando, not satisfied with his backheel, decided he would now score a volley. He spun his body, attempted a backflip, his leg went up but didn't pass the tipping point, came back, and the ball went in. Nobody understood anything. The image caused a tilt in every observer's brain. Contagio, who flew in from somewhere in the country and came straight from the airport just to watch the pelada, nearly choked from laughing so hard.

"It was a capoeira goal. The backflip didn't complete. The leg went but didn't pass. It came back. And it was a goal. Nobody understood anything." — Eyewitness

Chapter 7 — Wesley and the Penalty Collection

Wesley da Silva, the #1 player on the WoodenRichter Scale, the man the entire team voted as the best at everything, decided that tonight he would become the greatest penalty supplier in Woodenlegs history.

Penalty #1: Wesley, in goal, decides that being a goalkeeper is boring. He leaves the goal, dribbles past the entire team, advances all the way to the attack, loses the ball up front, and screams: "SOMEBODY SWITCH WITH ME!!" From the other side of the field, like a quantum-entangled particle, Big Mac teleports into goal and makes a save — with his hand. Italia swarms the referee. The ref caves to the pressure. Penalty. Guilherme converts.

Penalty #2: Wesley commits a violent foul on Barba outside the box. So ugly it became a penalty out of sheer disgust. The referee was nauseated. Guilherme converts.

Penalty #3: Wesley handles the ball inside the box. Classic. Simple. Efficient. Why complicate things? Guilherme converts.

Penalty #4 (post-game): After the match, at the beer table, Wesley committed yet another penalty. The details are confidential, but witnesses confirm he was venting something tonight.

Wesley also clobbered Barba twice. The second time, Barba stayed on the ground like a lizard — and it was Barba's own fault. If you lie on the field like a tropical reptile, someone is going to step on you. That's the law of nature.

Chapter 8 — Big Mac's Dream

Mac McClain — or McDonald's, as some have already christened him — fulfilled a childhood dream tonight. Ever since he was little, he watched Woodenlegs play and dreamed of one day wearing the shirt. Now that he's grown up, he finally got a spot on the team.

Wesley, ever the gracious host, welcomed him with plenty of complaints and open arms. But after the game ended, respect returned and everything was beautiful. Big Mac did well on his debut — he hustled hard and showed everyone how to play a fine game of... basketball? Well, two goals is two goals. Nobody can take that from him.

Chapter 9 — The Cast

Guilherme Souza — The man arrived at 19:34, half an hour late, head down, in total silence. And on the field he showed what he came for. Three goals. All penalties. All gifted by Wesley. Didn't even have to run. That's efficiency.

Arthur Mendes — Top scorer of the night with 4 goals. Two for Brasil, two on loan to Italia. Put Wesley on the ground early on. Grabbed Brocha from behind, they fell in a cuddle on the ground and stayed that way until Brocha decided to get up. Arthur doesn't discriminate: he takes down players from any team, including his own.

Brocha — Started the game thinking the teams were perfectly balanced (when his team was winning 5-1). When it was tied, he was furious the teams were unbalanced. When he won 13-11, he stopped complaining. Funny how that works.

Leeman Abdulai — Showed up wearing a gorgeous polo shirt, except for the color. It was somewhere between carrot vomit and fermented pumpkin juice. Nobody knew what color it was. Nobody had the courage to ask. Elegant, indefinable.

Eric Van Thorre — Ran more tonight than in the last three years combined. Saved up all his cardiovascular energy since 2023 to unleash a screamer into the top corner. And it worked. After that he never ran again.

Mano — Complained that the blue team was weaker. Then scored a goal and won. Life is unfair like that, Mano.

Fernando Nogueira (Ferpa) — Two historic goals: one backheel and one capoeira. Declared after the game that he played yesterday on a full-size pitch and that "it's way easier there, you don't have to run as much."

"What a workout this is. I'm dead." — Fernando Nogueira, 2026

Chapter 10 — The Audience

Carlos Moro arrived late after his traditional evening nap. Messed up the scoreboard because he showed up at exactly the wrong time.

Márcio Contagio, who worked in Seattle this week, caught a flight to wherever just to make it back in time to watch the pelada. Came straight from the airport. Sat on the sideline, watched Fernando's capoeira goal, and nearly didn't make it back to his flight from laughing so hard. From the group chat, he wrote: "They should have used this Italia team to qualify for the World Cup."

Chapter 11 — The Verdict

24 goals. 13 scorers. 3 penalties (all Wesley's fault). A goalkeeper who became a striker. A quantum particle in goal. A capoeira goal nobody understood. A debutant with 2 goals. A shirt the color of carrot vomit. And the most mathematically balanced game in Woodenlegs history — according to the WoodenRichter Scale, at least — that ended 11-13.

The AI picked the perfect teams. A difference of 0.02. And still, Joel and Mano complained.

See you next Friday.
